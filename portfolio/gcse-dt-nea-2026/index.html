The project

Making a donation box people would actually notice.

The brief began with a simple problem: physical charity boxes can be easy to overlook. I designed a tabletop donation box that makes giving a coin playful, explains the cause clearly, and connects physical interaction with online donations.

From brief to finished product

The strongest decisions came from testing ideas, finding what failed, and developing the design again.

Research and brief

Research showed that many donation boxes look generic and do little to explain the impact of a small donation. That led to a brief focused on visibility, trust, simple use, and a more engaging physical interaction.

Choosing the interaction

Two final concepts were compared: an information-led connected box and a basketball-style coin launcher. Feedback favoured the gamified concept, so it was developed with clearer messaging, a normal coin slot, and better accessibility.

Prototype and manufacture

Cardboard models exposed problems with the launcher, access door, coin path, and ergonomics. The final build used laser-cut plywood, comb joints, line-bent acrylic, a vacuum-formed backboard, a 3D-printed handle, and an oak stain.

Electronics and web

An ESP32 and ten LEDs gave the physical product a connected response to the mobile-first donation demo. The system was deliberately kept demonstrative: it showed the interaction without collecting real payments.

The finished outcome

A coin becomes a small challenge, not just a transaction.

Physical interaction

Users pull back a spring-powered launcher and aim a coin through the hoop. Missed coins return to the user for another attempt; successful coins are stored securely inside.

More than one way to give

A conventional coin slot keeps the product usable for anyone who does not want to play, while a QR-led mobile experience demonstrates a quick route for online donations.

Connected feedback

The ESP32-controlled LEDs run attention-catching patterns and can respond when the online demo records a donation, linking the digital and physical sides of the project.

Testing it honestly

The evaluation recorded both successful tests and the parts that should change in another iteration.

Passed

Every UK coin fitted

All standard UK coins worked with the launcher, conventional slot, and internal storage.

Passed

The light system stayed stable

All ten LEDs remained synchronised during a 30-minute continuous test, with no failed light patterns.

Learned

First use needed more guidance

Test users understood the launcher after a few attempts, but clearer instructions and a finger guide would make it easier immediately.

Limited

The plywood must stay indoors

Water testing caused the plywood to swell and roughen. A future version would need a waterproof finish or a different material.

What I would change next

  • Add a hoop sensor and RGB lighting for immediate feedback when a coin scores.
  • Enclose the underside electronics and reinforce the most heavily loaded joints.
  • Add clearer instructions, a visible coin window, and a more accessible launcher guide.
  • Use a water-resistant finish and design the mixed materials for easier repair and separation.
